So a \u201cno myth\u201d game was originally defined as a game where the game state wasn\u2019t fixed until it entered the shared imagined space.

That means that \u201cno myth\u201d is a property of the ontology of the game. Something exists if it\u2019s put into play and doesn\u2019t exist if it hasn\u2019t. (\u201cPut into play\u201d can also include things like \u201cOK, before we start, I want to say that this game is about Darth Vader in a shopping mall speaking french so those three things definitely exist\u201d.)

The GM in a no-myth game can think \u201cI really want to put this blue chair into play, I\u2019m gonna do it to the first normally furnished room the players enter, I feel such a strong attachment to this particular quantum blue chair. It\u2019s gonna get in for sure. Oh blue chair my favorite\u201d The fact that the GM is really into this blue chair does not make the blue chair half myth or the ontological state of the game \u201chalf myth\u201d.

This means, logically, that the phrase \u201chalf myth\u201d can\u2019t meaningfully be defined as \u201csome specific offscreen entities are canon, some are not\u201d. Because that\u2019s already what \u201cno myth\u201d means.

Instead, for a meaningful definition of \u201chalf myth\u201d, how about: \u201csome categories of offscreen entities are canon, some are not.\u201d

\u201cHi guys, I\u2019ll run everything in Duckburg fully prepped, but things over in Gooseburg is more \u2018no myth\u2019.\u201d

\u201cHi guys, everything related to combat is improvised and ran \u2018no myth\u2019, I\u2019ll just throw orcs atcha according to my own whim, but I have prepped all the entities and rules regarding the big fashion show in Veluna because I know that that\u2019s the aspect of play that we\u2019re the most invested in. Clothes, designers, audience reactions\u2014all accounted for & implemented. Only with a steady whimsical stream of orc with murderous intent.\u201d

What does this mean for Burning Wheel? It\u2019s more of a no-myth game innit? There is no category of entities in the game that can be consistently expected to be solid.

    You are saying: "look how much better play is when you have rules and metarules for how to introduce elements! Here are my rules and metarules and they are so great!"

    And we are all, like, "Hell yeah! Those are awesome!" [And, of course, you're presenting them in a different - and, I would argue, better - way, but they're rules and metarules that lots of people and play traditions have used before. But I'll just call them "yours" as a shorthand for now.]

If you do that, then people playing that way (which is a lot of people just now!) can go play and then notice that same problem and understand what you were saying (and change how they play and potentially join your crusade ).
