Story of my two wasted decades!

I started with some very crappy and bad roleplaying games. Some 90s games.

I had never seen a module, a location-based module. I had no idea that such a thing could exist.

I had seen railroad- or funnel- type adventure books. These absolutely sucked because the players zagged off them within three seconds.

Sometimes books mentioned prepping a game world and I was like \u201cPrepping every grain of sand on the entire planet, that\u2019s not reasonable, no, no, no!\u201d (Similar to how some people reacted in my other thread recently). Because I hadn\u2019t seen the tools that the OSR community cooked up (some of these weren\u2019t new).

I was like \u201cWait, do they really mean that you can just cheat and railroad and force and do all kinds of shit back here behind this screen? Why don\u2019t you just improvise everything then? Why do you need all of these heavy rules and all these heavy traditional techniques?\u201d (Also in the 90s, games were super complicated & cumbersome. Everything was like \u201coh this is a Mental Hard Skill it costs 12 character points to buy to the level of your attribute +2\u201d and I was like uh\u2026 I\u2019m just a li\u2019l kid and also math is hard.) First three or four years wasted on just being confused!!! Everyone was like \u201cSandra, don\u2019t even look at D&D, it sucks, what is AC anyway, you dodge better in plate are you serious? What\u2019s a class and level, you graduate fighter school in the middle of the dungeon?\u201d)

And then I found Everway and Fudge/SLUG to be like \u201chey girl relax you can play these kindsa game more easily\u201d. Some ten years of pretty happy & working gaming! Dropping the dice&cards after a while. Some really memorable sessions that I\u2019ll treasure always. But\u2026 I had one player who was really into this type of gaming but everyone else dropped out and was Not Into It. I had a really hard time keeping players around and I didn\u2019t know why and maybe they didn\u2019t know either but\u2026

I believe now why they didn\u2019t like it is that the agency was super low. I made pregens, I \u201cimprovised\u201d the stories, the outcomes of their actions\u2026 the one player that it grooved with was skilled at switching stances on a moment\u2019s notice. For example, he made a character that could switch minds and memories with other characters, so when he stole the memories of the NPC, I could be like \u201cAnd now, it\u2019s time for you to reveal your big secret plan!\u201d and he, as the NPC, would reveal a perfectly grand secret plan that that NPC could\u2019ve created. Stance hopping. I trusted him with this and when I didn\u2019t extend that same trust to other players, they dropped out. When I did extend the same trust to other players, they froze in the headlights & dropped out.

Then , at a party, someone told me about an anecdote from a game. Something about a stolen helicopter and a one in two hundred chance that the PCs could fly it correctly with no skill and them flying it for a while\u2014longer than anyone expected\u2014until crashing it and dying. I was like \u201cWait, that could never happen in my happy rule-less/dice-less game. [They would\u2019ve just said \u201cnuh-uh, we don\u2019t die\u201d and/or I would\u2019ve been too invested in their success to put them in real harms way] I need to bring back rules!\u201d

Then followed seven years or so (roughly) of absolutely miserable and awful gaming! Flailing around needing blorb but not having any idea about blorb or that anything like blorb could exist! Remember, I had never seen a location-based module. I read like 200 core books, the occasional railroady adventure book or campaign book, being like\u2026 \u201cbut these games are half games, there are rules for the PCs but where are the rules for the GM/Storyteller/Keeper?\u201d

There are posts on S-G from me from that period where I\u2019m applying this to Lady Blackbird and Cthulhu Dark (the latter though isn\u2019t \u201chalf a game\u201d because it does have the \u201cUse these rules to play modules\u201d clause. It\u2019s just that I hadn\u2019t seen any of these modules\u2014the CoC / ToC adventures that I had stumbled over where railroady).

There\u2019s a thread on The Big Purple where I\u2019m arguing with a GURPS fan and I\u2019m being so stupid (from the POV of someone who knows that prep and blorb are things). I\u2019m discussing the jumping rules which are\u2026 kinda complicated\u2026 you can jump (lowest of [(2 \xd7 [BM + yards ran before jump]) - 3 feet], (2 \xd7 [(2 \xd7 [BM]) - 3 feet])), where \u201cBM\u201d is floor([(HT+DX)/4] + [increments of five character points spent during creation]). (You can see why I kinda bounced off it as a kid. And why it lead me to having to learn a bunch of math and stuff later in life. Which is boring af.) And I\u2019m like OK, cool, cool, where are the rules for how wide chasms the GM can put in there? I couldn\u2019t find them? And the guy, the GURPS fan is like \u201cthe GM can just decide freely the chasm widths\u201d and I\u2019m like whiskey tango actual foxtrot? Because I didn\u2019t understand how prep could work. I had never seen a location based module. I was all character points and \u201cfairness\u201d and\u2026 and\u2026

Here is why \u201cNo Paper After Seeing Rock\u201d principle starts to make sense, too, right? Placing chasms after character creation does feel a bit\u2026

At this point I\u2019m also doing Fiasco (which works great\u2014like the good games with the stance-switching-skilled guy except more people are being able to grok it because the game explains itself way better than I ever did, and, more importantly, we\u2019re less tied to my conception of how the story should unfold) and Fate (which works not so great because I have no diegetic coherence and also the game is imbalanced af, sometimes they breeze through an army of death robots and sometimes they lose horribly to a tiny li\u2019l werewolf) and then bam. First few months of 2013, I think it was. [So way late to the party compared to Grognardia and the OSR community which, at that point, had grown a large presence even in my own city.] \u201cMirror story\u201d happens. I\u2019m like whaaaat. This is what I\u2019ve been looking for all my life!

It\u2019s even possible that I had played in some fully blorby games [with rando passing-through GMs] before that, I remember a WoD game that was awesome or a CoC game that was unusally tense. But I didn\u2019t know they were blorby. I was like \u201cWhat are those rules the DM is using to create this stuff? All of this could just as easily be improvised, right? Is it?\u201d Playing in The Lost City not knowing that it was blorby or what blorb was \u201cbut an unsually cool session\u201d, reading the module [seeing how much "there" there was], and then shortly thereafter playing in the \u201cmirror story\u201d game\u2026 just finally getting it.

    That was also my reaction when I read the \u201cno myth\u201d thread on Forge\u2026 \u201cYou mean like I\u2019ve been playing for the last ten years already?\u201d

    I want to teach and explain blorb & gloracle, especially to new DMs, because I know for a fact that some people do not know that it exists, because I was one.

    Now, a lot of GMs saw success in the seventies and eighties by compromising blorb a little bit, here and there. Fudging, improvising, incorporating player ideas. It is completely understandable how this happened, why they saw early success, and how that lead to\u2026 the 90s\u2026

    (From this perspective of gaming history the word \u201crenaissance\u201d is really appropriate.)

    You\u2019re in a 90s muck and system stops mattering and it\u2019s all\u2026 and then people were like \u201cthis ain\u2019t working. The hobby is shrinking af and it\u2019s just\u2026\u201d so in comes\u2026 on the one hand, the forge dorks. \u201cHey system needs to start mattering again, how can we make it more coherent, how can we find rules for the entire procedure of play, not just for the \u2018players\u2019\u201d and on the other hand, the OSR dorks, saying\u2026 pretty much the exact same thing. (Mixed in with some other cargo cult artifacts that got bricolaged in there such as \u201crulings not rules\u201d, \u201cclassic pulp tropes\u201d, \u201cthe more gonzo the better\u201d etc.)

    (I\u2019ve said before that obv \u201crulings not rules\u201d and games like Searchers of the Unknown was a reaction to the lopsidedness of some 90s games like GURPS [I know it was published in 1985]. Maybe an overreaction; the problem wasn\u2019t that there were rules for jumping (which Searchers of the Unknown doesn\u2019t have at all), the problem was the quantum play culture.)

    It\u2019s also understandable how I, from the railroady adventure books, couldn\u2019t deduce the existence of blorb.

    These books are a collection of events. \u201cFirst the players go here and do this, then they go here and do this, and then they go here and do this. If they don\u2019t go here and don\u2019t do this, you can improv a bit until you\u2019re back on track.\u201d It\u2019s either obvious that the \u201cevents\u201d are suggestions [in which case they teach nothing about tangibility/solidity/blorb], or you take them rigidly in which case you\u2019re gonna come down hard on agency.

    I only gave these types of adventures a handful of tries, generally sticking to complete improvisation instead. [In my non-agential, crappy improvisation b/c stance issues didn\u2019t lead me to incorporate player ideas in a consistent way. It\u2019s not that I couldn\u2019t improvise and (content warning: extreme gore, horror) do what the guy is doing in this video, I can, easily. It\u2019s that I didn\u2019t do it in a way that was agential.

    It\u2019s all still just hat-pullery and \u201cmaybe this happens, maybe that happens, idk\u201d and \u201cit\u2019s true because I say so, that\u2019s why it\u2019s true\u201d. You can see how something like the \u201cmirror story\u201d would be mind blowing to me in that context. How, comparatively, \u201creal\u201d it felt.

    \nI was like the two kids in this chess story but for roleplaying games.

    I had a similar way of thinking as le Joueur did when he came up with the "no myth" philosophy.

    When the mirror story happened, that was a\u2026 can you imagine? Can you imagine the two chess kids in that story going on for twenty years and then one day stumbling over a chess board? Wait, what, there is a board? I was utterly and thoroughly shocked at the experience!

    And\u2026 I was not alone in being unaware of, or not believing in, the board. I go on forums and try to teach this stuff or explain this stuff and they're like "stfu you timecube kook, go back to the streets with your "end is nigh" sign, just leave us alone with your crazy theories". And that is wearing me down.
