The basics

So this is a thread that people can link back to when they are confused about diegetics, diegetical, diegesis etc.

Yes, this is a weird word.

But it\u2019s easily explained by saying that it\u2019s opposite to things like in real life, out of character etc.

Let\u2019s say a game group is sitting in mom\u2019s kitchen and they playing a game set in the magical realm of Gondor. The clock on the wall in mom\u2019s kitchen says it\u2019s eight PM. The time in the magical realm of Gondor is just at the crack of dawn, outside the bandit camp. The diegetical time is just at the crack of dawn, not eight PM.\n

Contrasted to \u201cDice\u201d

Related concepts, sometimes conflated (including by me #SloppyAcademic2097 ) are the Shared Imagined Space (the \u201cSIS\u201d) and the \u201cCloud\u201d, the latter most prominently used in @lumpley \u2019s dice and cloud series from ten years ago; which contrasted it with \u201cdice\u201d, a word that means not only dice but also character sheets, numbers, pencils, special abilities, hit points, stats etc.\n

Contrasted to mimesis

In classical theatre, and in literary criticism, diegesis (which, in that context, means \u201cnarrated stuff\u201d) is contrasted to mimesis, which, in that context, means \u201cshown stuff\u201d \u2014 \u201cmimicking\u201d is based on the same word, it means imitation).

In many other media, including tabletop RPG and movies, this distinction has often fallen away and the distinction isn\u2019t really being made (including by me #SloppyAcademic2097 ).

The first time I heard the word \u201cdiegetical\u201d it was in the context of \u201cdiegetical music is movie music that both the audience and the movie characters hear, maybe they have the radio on in the kitchen and dance to it; extradiegetical music is movie music that only the audience hear \u2014 subtle romantic notes as the two main leads first see each other\u201d.

Arguably if people are LARPing out game scenes and showing each other what they do, that\u2019s mimesis rather than diegesis. So maybe that\u2019s a distinction that will be brought back in the future but if you\u2019re digging through old threads don\u2019t worry about it, it\u2019s all the same fuzzy fancy way of saying \u201cin-game\u201d.\n

Why not just say \u201cin-game\u201d then?

This is kind of a selfish reason but the first couple of times I was legit confused. I mistakenly though this: We are sitting around a game table in a game room and we are a game group; aren\u2019t all of our activities, including mom\u2019s wall clock, \u201cin-game\u201d? And, similarly, the \u201cdice\u201d stuff (see above) is definitely part of the game activity but is still not necessarily diegetical.

So I needed a specific, dedicated word and the phrase from the sound track industry came to mind. (This was like decades ago.)

I later learned of the terms \u201ccloud\u201d and the \u201cSIS\u201d and sometimes use those words instead. Sorry about that.\u2665\n

Update about the term \u201cSIS\u201d

In the past, I\u2019ve used the term SIS a lot for the diegesis, like, a lot, and I can\u2019t rellay go back and edit all those posts, but starting now, I\u2019ll stop using it. Here\u2019s where I went wrong.

I\u2019ve learned (in this thread) that people are really hung up on the \u201cShared\u201d part of the term SIS (Shared Imagined Space). Re-reading old Forge stuff, they\u2019re not wrong. But, I\u2019ll explain myself.

When I heard the word \u201cShared\u201d, I thought of the Swedish word \u201cgemensam\u201d. Something you have together. Me and my sister can share a book even though I haven\u2019t read all of it. Wiktionary says:\n
Wiktionary said:

Adjective

shared (not comparable)
Used by multiple entities or for multiple purposes or in multiple ways. quotations

Synonyms

  • (multiple entities): common, mutual
Now, I\u2019m not trying to do an \u201cwell, actually, the dictionary says so-and-so, so that\u2019s what you meant when you said shared\u201d. Uh, no. If that\u2019s not what they meant, it\u2019s not what they meant. But\u2026 it\u2019s what I meant when using the word shared imagined space up until the last week or so.

In my view of the phrase \u201cSIS\u201d, I thought it meant \u201cOK, we have this imagined space together. It\u2019s shared by us. The red ball in the chest is part of this shared imagined space even though the chest hasn\u2019t been opened so people don\u2019t know that there\u2019s a red ball in there.\u201d

What some of the Forge nerds sometimes meant by \u201cShared\u201d was specifically things that have been shared in sense four in this definition, while I thought they meant sense two. Which to me with my background in semiotics, sense two would\u2019ve made a hell of a lot more sense!

But in this sense-four \u201cShared Imagined Space\u201d, the red ball is not part of the SIS because it hasn\u2019t been told-about (\u201cshared\u201d sense four) even though it\u2019s a relevant part of our common imagination (sense two).

Through these last few weeks of debate, I\u2019ve, confusingly enough, gradually shifted my use of SIS over to the Forge \u201csense four\u201d usage in order to contrast it with my own use of \u201cdiegesis\u201d and \u201ccanon\u201d and \u201cgamestate\u201d and/or whatever we\u2019re calling it today since we apparently can\u2019t call it SIS because of this \u201csense-four-only usage\u201d.

    Now, we play the game. The PCs enter the room with the vorpal sword, and the GM starts describing the room... but then someone says, "Hang on! We forgot to roll for a random encounter!"

    Oh no! We pause that narration (no sword has been described yet), and we roll. A green dragon comes up! The PCs fight the dragon and many cool things happen. The GM is about to get back to describing the contents of the room, including the vorpal sword, but then the phone rings and someone has to go to work early! We agree to wrap up the campaign there, and then, sadly, by the time out next session comes around, the GM has moved to Florida (why? oh why?). So we continue playing, but with Julian GMing, instead, and we embark on an entirely different adventure, having left those caverns behind.

    Now imagine an alternative version, where there is a Reaction Check (this particular group plays old-school!), and the green dragon gets a Very Friendly result. Thinking this over, Bob the Fighter realizes that his father had mentioned something about rescuing a green dragon hatchling 50 years ago! Oh, wow! Surely, this might be *that very dragon*! Otherwise, how do you explain a green dragon suddenly being all friendly? It must be because it recognizes Bob's heritage! (Maybe he smells like his dad, or something.)

    Everyone's laughing, and the idea is so clear and so *right* that no one dares ruin the moment: clearly this is what the gloracle has said.

    So!

    As far as the prep is concerned, the vorpal sword was "there", right? And was there a friendly green dragon wandering around the dungeon? Not in the same sense as the sword, not at all.

    But, as far as what happened diegetically/in the fiction/at the table/in the SIS, the friendly-dragon-that-Bob's-dad-rescued-long-ago is far more "real" than the vorpal sword, which, as far as we are concerned, never existed at all.       Update about the term \u201cSIS\u201d

      In the past, I\u2019ve used the term SIS a lot for the diegesis, like, a lot, and I can\u2019t rellay go back and edit all those posts, but starting now, I\u2019ll stop using it. Here\u2019s where I went wrong.

      I\u2019ve learned (in this thread) that people are really hung up on the \u201cShared\u201d part of the term SIS (Shared Imagined Space). Re-reading old Forge stuff, they\u2019re not wrong. But, I\u2019ll explain myself.

      When I heard the word \u201cShared\u201d, I thought of the Swedish word \u201cgemensam\u201d. Something you have together. Me and my sister can share a book even though I haven\u2019t read all of it. Wiktionary says:\n
      Wiktionary said:

      Adjective

      shared (not comparable)
      Used by multiple entities or for multiple purposes or in multiple ways. quotations

      Synonyms

      • (multiple entities): common, mutual
      Now, I\u2019m not trying to do an \u201cwell, actually, the dictionary says so-and-so, so that\u2019s what you meant when you said shared\u201d. Uh, no. If that\u2019s not what they meant, it\u2019s not what they meant. But\u2026 it\u2019s what I meant when using the word shared imagined space up until the last week or so.

      In my view of the phrase \u201cSIS\u201d, I thought it meant \u201cOK, we have this imagined space together. It\u2019s shared by us. The red ball in the chest is part of this shared imagined space even though the chest hasn\u2019t been opened so people don\u2019t know that there\u2019s a red ball in there.\u201d

      \n Lemme try:
      A scene is a situation playing out in a location. In games that lean into narrative, a scene is generally completed by the resolution of a key procedural intention, a dramatic gambit or a "beat" (ie, an emotionally significant measure of progress or setback), thereby resolving the situation in one way or another. In some systems (DramaSystem, for example) the gambit or intention is stated ahead of playing it out, making it somewhat easier to tell when the scene has reached its end.

      In games that lean away from narrativism, it's probably easier to consider the scene's end to be the point at which the subject(s) of the scene leave the location and go to another.

      ETA: It is possible - though not common - for a scene to cross more than one location. In this case the narrative model is almost certainly being employed (think "training montage" for instance), and thus the scene is not necessarily limited by geographic concerns.

      \n No interpretation with my definition of a scene provided characters are finite beings and a location is delimited.
